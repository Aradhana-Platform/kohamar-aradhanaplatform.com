Often much is made about the difference between these two words and their meanings,<sup>3</sup> but in this context, they are interchangeable.<sup>4</sup> Moreover, as Kenneth Mathews rightly argues, even the prepositions <em>bᵉ</em> with <em>tselem</em> and <em>kᵉ</em> with <em>demut</em> are interchangeable.<sup>5</sup> The best place for understanding the meaning of a text is its immediate context, and in the context of Genesis 1, the image of God is linked with the divine command to subdue and rule (imperative forms of the two verbs are used in v. 28) the rest of creation (cf. “and over all the earth” in v. 26). Hence, whatever else image/likeness of God in humans may mean, at the very least it means that humans have the divine mandate to subdue and rule over God’s beautiful creation.<sup>6</sup> Mathews sees humans in Gen 1:26 as royal representatives of God, especially in light of the verb <em>rdh</em>.<sup>7</sup>
